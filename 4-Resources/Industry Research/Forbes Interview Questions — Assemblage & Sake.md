# FORBES INTERVIEW — HEAVENSAKE Q&A

*Source: Forbes-Interview-Questions-13.pdf — Full interview Q&A with Carl Hirschmann on Assemblage and HEAVENSAKE's history*

---

## Q: Can you share the journey of bringing the Assemblage technique to the world of sake?

The first brewery we collaborated with on this project was Dassai. Our agreement with them was straightforward. They understood the concept and how it worked in champagne making, but they were unsure how it would translate to the world of sake. They told us, "You're welcome to come and experiment with our sake, but we can only move forward if the result not only tastes good but is also significantly different from what we already produce." It was crucial that any sommelier or experienced sake professional could immediately recognize that the product was distinct. If it were only marginally different, it would compromise the credibility of not just our project, but also their involvement.

I like to say that the true birth of the HEAVENSAKE project was when Régis traveled to Dassai for the first assemblage session. It took a few days for the Toji master and the Dassai team to fully embrace his presence. However, once they understood his approach and saw the result, they were completely captivated. From that point, they were fully committed to working with us. That was a pivotal step forward.

With other breweries, the process has followed a similar pattern. We first reach out to gauge their interest in collaborating. However, there's often a gap between those making the decisions at a higher management level and the sake brewers on the ground. As a result, we have to convince both parties — first, the owners or executives, and then the Toji, the sake master. This is important not only for logistical reasons but also as a matter of principle.

Every time, the initial reaction is one of surprise, but by the end, they are completely won over. The power of assemblage to enhance and elevate flavors is something that can accelerate their progress by years. Typically, in sake brewing, if you want to adjust the flavor profile, you're constrained by certain elements, and you may have to wait for the next brewing season to experiment further. It's a lengthy process of trial and error. However, with assemblage, you can explore a vast range of options in a much shorter time. When you know how to combine flavors and aromas effectively, it opens up entirely new possibilities.

## Q: What inspired you to introduce this winemaking concept to HEAVENSAKE?

The concept of a cultural exchange between Japan and France has always been at the heart of the HEAVENSAKE project. First, it stemmed from a group of French enthusiasts who recognized the potential of Japanese sake and sought to contribute to the sake industry. Additionally, the deep historical and cultural ties between France and Japan played a significant role. Our two countries are not only economically linked but also share a profound mutual respect.

We also aimed to influence the taste of sake. It was essential for us to ask, "How can we bring a touch of French joie de vivre, that French savoir-faire, to sake — the same qualities that make French wines and champagnes so globally coveted?" We wanted to delve into the essence of what creates these unique flavors and profiles.

In exploring this, it became clear that in the world of champagne, it is the technique of assemblage that unlocks these distinct qualities. Thus, the key question became: how impactful would this technique be when applied to sake?

## Q: How did your collaboration with sake master Naohiko Noguchi come about, and what was it like working together on the Noguchi Prestige sake?

Every industry has its leading figures, its icons, and its legendary stories. Just as Régis Camus is the most awarded and recognized champagne maker — having won the IWC title eight times, more than anyone else — Noguchi Naohiko holds a similar status in the sake world. Often referred to as the "God of Sake," Noguchi-san's story is extraordinary, still brewing sake at the age of 92. The idea of bringing these two grandmasters together — one in his nineties and the other in his seventies — was truly a dream come true.

We were fortunate that Noguchi-san agreed to the collaboration. When Régis and I met him, the mutual respect between the two masters made the process seamless. Noguchi-san was well aware of Régis' achievements, and Régis, of course, knew the immense legacy that Noguchi-san carried within the sake industry.

Despite their legendary status, both men were remarkably humble and easy to work with. When professionals of such caliber meet, and there is a shared dedication to precision and excellence, the collaboration flows naturally. There was no ego involved; on the contrary, it was a truly pleasant experience.

Although they did not speak the same language, they understood each other perfectly. The process was almost magical.

We documented the collaboration, and as we neared the final stages of assemblage — just one or two iterations away from the perfect blend — both Régis and Noguchi-san, without consulting each other, instinctively knew what needed to be done to achieve the final blend.

This experience was remarkable because it demonstrated how deeply they both understood the process, the evolution of the liquid, and what was required to reach the perfect outcome.

## Q: Assemblage in wine focuses on enhancing each component's qualities. How does this approach differ when applied to sake compared to traditional sake blending methods?

It's crucial to differentiate between blending and assemblage. In traditional sake-making, blending was primarily used for practical purposes — harmonizing batches, optimizing costs, and managing stock. Essentially, it was a way to combine different batches without any intention of enhancing the flavor or unlocking new taste profiles. Moreover, blending was seldom done with high-end sake, so the idea of applying it to premium sake initially felt almost blasphemous. These are, after all, the creations of the Toji masters, and there was skepticism about whether it would bring any added value. Even John Gauntner, a renowned sake expert, was initially doubtful, as he saw it through the lens of traditional blending.

Assemblage, however, is a completely different approach. In champagne-making, assemblage involves two different techniques: one for brut, which focuses on creating a steady, harmonious taste — closer to what one might call blending — and the other, more complex form of assemblage, which is about elevating and creating new flavor combinations. It's much like mixing colors in painting. If you know what you're doing, you can achieve vibrant, unique colors that didn't exist before. Some of the greatest painters are celebrated for unlocking these kinds of colors, and you can see it very clearly when you mix blue and yellow to create green.

Similarly, in sake assemblage, we're not just combining shades of blue to create a consistent batch. We're asking, "How can we take these colors and create a vibrant, unique purple that stands out in a way no one else could achieve?" This is what makes assemblage special — it's about elevation and innovation.

This is also why Régis Camus has won the IWC title eight times. When people taste his creations blindly, they can immediately tell: "This is just different. This is better." In sake-making, the challenge was determining how much of an impact assemblage could have. We would start with existing sakes — typically five to ten different types — and begin the process of trial and error, much like alchemists, to see what combinations would harmonize and elevate the flavor.

Sometimes, just adding or removing half a percent of a liquid can create a completely different taste, which is truly mind-blowing. For example, when we invited John Gauntner to one of these sessions, the outcome was undeniable — he was completely astonished by the results and became a supporter of the project.

## Q: What were some of the initial challenges or misconceptions you encountered when introducing Assemblage to the sake industry, and how did you overcome them?

From the moment we realized that assemblage worked, especially during our initial work with Dassai, there weren't any significant challenges. Of course, there were skeptics we had to convince over time, but that's a normal part of introducing something new. We never saw it as a challenge, but rather as a natural process to go through.

From a technical perspective, however, there were occasionally difficulties, particularly because our taste profile is very specific. Régis has very high standards when it comes to achieving the perfect length in the sake.

The identity of HEAVENSAKE's assemblage is built around four main criteria: a **fruity palate, a floral nose, a silky mouthfeel, and an extended finish**. Achieving this perfect length can sometimes be tricky, and if it isn't right, Régis is not afraid to start again from scratch. It's not uncommon for him to arrive at the perfect taste with great mouthfeel and aroma, but if the length isn't there, he'll reset the process. That's where the technical challenge lies.

Another challenge we've encountered is addressing the misconception between blending and assemblage, something that we're still working to communicate effectively. We've come to realize that educating people on this distinction is crucial.

Interestingly, if you visit the most high-end sake shops in Japan today, you'll notice that several very expensive bottles — ranging from $500 to $1,000 — now proudly display "assemblage" on their labels. It has become a concept the sake industry recognizes and uses as a marketing and sales tool to enhance their premium positioning. We're proud to say that we were the first to introduce assemblage to high-end sake, and it's rewarding to see the industry embrace its beauty.

## Q: HEAVENSAKE's new releases include sake crafted with Katsuyama, Niizawa, and Noguchi breweries. How did you adapt the Assemblage technique to respect the traditions and uniqueness of each brewery?

That's actually a straightforward process because we start with the existing sakes of the brewery we're collaborating with. Each sake has its own identity and provides us with a foundation to work from. In that sense, we are somewhat confined to what already exists, but that's part of the beauty — it's a sign of respect for the original product and also presents a unique technical challenge, as we work within those boundaries.

The process itself is immensely enjoyable, and we have documented it through videos that show the evolution. It's always an interesting journey. On the first day, the Toji masters are usually somewhat cautious, perhaps uncertain about what is happening. But by the end of the process, they are completely on board, fully supporting the project.

There's a moment of true joy and satisfaction when the final blend is achieved, and everyone in the room unanimously agrees that this is the perfect expression. Sometimes, the process takes days of experimentation, but when that moment comes, it's unmistakable. The sense of fulfillment and accomplishment shared by everyone involved is really quite beautiful.

## Q: The artistic ceramic bottle for the limited-edition sake is crafted by Arita Porcelain Lab. How important is the visual and artistic aspect of sake presentation in complementing the product inside?

For us, it is absolutely essential to create harmony between the visual and artistic aspects of the product and the liquid itself, which also carries an artistic touch through the process of assemblage and Régis' expertise. When you look at the history of alcohol brands that have transcended their categories — such as Absolut Vodka or Patrón Tequila — one thing they all have in common is iconic design. People form emotional connections with products, particularly with premium alcohol brands. You want these products in your fridge, on your table, and in your hand. The object itself becomes essential.

That said, an aesthetically pleasing object cannot compensate for a subpar liquid in the long term. At the same time, even if you have an exceptional liquid, if it's housed in an uninspiring bottle, it becomes difficult to gain traction or make an impact. What truly matters is a perfect combination of both — a compelling visual identity and an outstanding liquid.

The total export value of sake from Japan to the rest of the world is only around **$300 million**. Given the beauty, history, and potential of this magnificent drink, that number should be three to five times higher. So the question becomes: how do we achieve that? It's clear what it takes, because we've seen it done before. Tequila, for example, is now a household name, but there was a time when it wasn't even on the global map.

It took specific brands, backed by influential people who were dedicated to product quality and storytelling, to elevate tequila to where it is today. They invested in marketing and shared the beauty of the product with the world. That's exactly what we're trying to do with HEAVENSAKE.

## Q: As you embark on this U.S. tour with HEAVENSAKE, what do you hope to achieve in terms of educating consumers about the relationship between winemaking and sake brewing?

There are the obvious parallels between sake and wine — their beautiful histories, similar flavor profiles, and the dedication of the craftsmen behind both. However, one of the most important aspects for us to communicate is the use of a wine glass when drinking sake.

It's astonishing to me that even in Japan, at the most high-end omakase restaurants — sake is still often served in traditional ochoko cups. Anyone who has done a taste test with us will immediately notice a difference, not only in the mouthfeel but, most significantly, in the aroma. The ochoko simply doesn't allow you to experience the full range of sake's fragrances and aromas.

By using a wine glass, you can truly appreciate the complexity of the assemblage and all the subtle notes of high-end sake. Drinking from a traditional sake cup causes you to miss out on roughly 30% of the experience — it's akin to drinking fine wine from a regular cup where you can't smell its bouquet. The entire sensory experience changes. The taste is different, the aroma is different, and overall, the enjoyment is diminished.

As we expand into the Japanese market, this is something we're really focusing on. By encouraging the use of wine glasses, we can enhance the entire experience of drinking sake. And as we know, in today's world, the experience is everything.
