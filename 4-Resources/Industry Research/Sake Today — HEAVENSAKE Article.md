# SAKE TODAY — HEAVENSAKE FEATURE

*Title: "Harmonies of Flavour"*

## ARTICLE TEXT (FULL)

Heavensake is proof of how far a private label brand can go when all the right elements are in place. The French-inspired, Japanese-produced line of sake is popping up everywhere from Europe's trendiest clubs and events to retailers of all colors across America. Its momentum is still building too.

A large driver of its success is the person behind the sake: Régis Camus. Or rather, we should say the person behind the assemblage — the blending of the sake. Camus is world renowned for having been a long tenured chef-de-cave at Piper-Heidsieck, the Champagne house established in 1785. Most would consider this the peak of a stellar career in the alcohol beverage industry, but as Camus approached retirement age — for normal folks, at least — he began delving into sake.

What could have led to this surprising shift as opposed to simply letting his considerable legacy rest? Three unusual friends with rich experience, passion, and an idea. That's how Heavensake began.

Carl Hirschmann, Etienne Russo, and Benjamin Eymere are the three friends behind the brand's conception. As Hirschmann, the Creative Director, tells the story in a lengthy interview together with Camus, "It happened by chance. We all figured out we loved sake. Our first discussions about doing something together were about twelve years ago, but the moment when things like that become serious takes a bit of time. We believed sake had enormous potential, but thought that if we were going to bring something to sake, we needed to have a real story, not just, 'Hey, a few guys went and created a new bottle.' Because we were all French, we thought we could do something similar to what Grey Goose did with vodka — try to add a bit of French joie de vivre to sake."

This was certainly a team equipped to launch the idea. Hirschmann is a successful entrepreneur who additionally understood the world of alcohol beverages like a map-maker knows the lay of the land. Etienne Russo is one of the world's most lauded fashion producers, working with brands like Chanel and Hermès. Benjamin Eymere is a media mogul and the CEO of L'Officiel. Yes, it helped that they all loved sake, but a launch needs fuel.

Their original idea was to have the sake made in France, but that evolved into the concept of using sake produced in Japan by esteemed breweries. It furthermore needed to be "designed," so to speak, by somebody in their world. They needed a blender, an individual with impeccable experience and success with champagne.

The friends began their search. It turns out that one of them had a familial connection with Piper-Heidsieck's owner and were able to connect with Camus. The concerned parties proceeded with delicate professionalism. Camus, after all, was already employed.

He agreed to help them in his free time, without any specific terms of a deal, telling them, "I want to enjoy this experience with you, and if it becomes a success, then I'm happy to later join as a shareholder and cofounder."

The "experience" was what they had hoped for, eventually leading to him becoming an integral part of Heavensake. The resume Camus brought was more than simply his previous place of employment and the successes that the company achieved. He needed a wheelbarrow to carry all his awards.

He was IWC's "Sparkling Winemaker of the Year" eight times. With a palate and blending skills of that caliber, it was only a matter of time before he would be impressing brewery owners and toji alike with what they never realized was possible with their own sake.

Camus describes his craft of assemblage in intriguing terms. As we've noted before, blending is not new to sake breweries, but it's usually done with the intent of achieving consistency between batches and/or brewing years.

Camus explains, "The first principal is that one plus one does not equal two, but much more. It's like putting together different pieces in a musical composition. They have to support each other and create the right balance. What's going to bring out fruitiness? Floral notes? What's going to yield a silky mouthfeel?"

He also emphasises long en bouche, or "length on the palate" — the extent to which the flavour of the sake lingers. Camus has spent hours blending sake that produces results where the nose, the mouthfeel, and the flavour were all amazing, but the length, too short. He starts over.

"Assemblage is creating a composition of elements," he concludes, "that complete each other so that it's harmonious and rounded, but also aligned with the essence and identity of that sake style."

Camus' introduction to sake was through formal tastings where, at one point, he got to taste high-end sake.

"It was quite a surprise because what I was used to until then was sake with a harsh aftertaste. I think, for me, that was the moment of 'discovery.' It was about fifteen years ago and, unfortunately, I don't recall the exact sake, just the moment itself."

Camus is one of several illustrious beverage professionals in France who have gone all in on sake following such a discovery. We've recently featured Richard Geoffroy of IWA sake and Xavier Thuizat of Hôtel de Crillon.

What is it about sake or Japan that attracts such talent in France?

Camus remembers a period about three or four decades ago when there was a robust exchange between the countries. French chefs went to Japan, and Japanese chefs went to France, thus creating common appreciation of their cuisines. Furthermore, in 1970 and 1990 disease devastated French oyster farms and Japanese oyster farmers came to the rescue with larvae so that they could recover.

The French returned the favour after March 11th, 2011 when the tsunami from the Great East Japan Earthquake wiped out the infrastructure of the ports throughout Japan's northeast, where oyster farming is a key industry. The French sent all kinds of equipment, from buoys and ropes to clothing and other essential things.

"There are beautiful stories of collaborations and support between the two countries," Camus says. "And I remember the intensity of interest on trips to promote champagne. Of all the places I was going around the world, nobody had such depth of interest in champagne making as the Japanese. It was real admiration. Things like this have created strong bonds. Naturally, the French have discovered this wonderful beverage, sake. It's always been based on mutual interest and respect."

Now, Camus together with his partners at Heavensake are hoping to give back to Japan and the sake industry by working with breweries there and elevating the profile of sake worldwide.

"I purposely advise people to drink from a wine glass — a big one with ample volume. And I recommend a slightly higher temperature so that our sake is in the range of a champagne or white wine. I feel that it's really like this — drinking it like a wine — that you can best appreciate it. That's how you draw out the floral and fruity character on the nose, how you allow the aromas to develop. Through assemblage, I create aromas that can evolve in a glass with proper temperatures. In an ordinary ochoko (cylindrical sake cup), you can't fully appreciate the aromas. You are removing thirty to forty percent of the experience. Imagine drinking wine without using your nose! Your nose is important, and part of assemblage is creating that appeal. So to your question, it's not really so much a marketing strategy as the simple reality of things; it's the facts of what I experience. That dictates to me the right way to appreciate sake."

His results have been impressing the upper echelons of the industry. You don't get to work with some of the breweries Heavensake has engaged with if you don't bring convincing results and a solid plan for branding. Those breweries obviously care about their own branding and that extends to their association with what outside groups they partner with on a private-label product.

Heavensake started out working with Dassai for its Junmai Daiginjo, which won awards in 2019 and 2020 on the heels of its release at the San Francisco IWC. Its partnership soon broadened to include Urakasumi and Konishi. The awards continued.

Its current producer-partners (at time of publication) are Urakasumi, Konishi, Tatenokawa, and Katsuyama. They had a collaboration planned with Niizawa.

Anecdotally, one toji among the breweries Heavensake works with proclaimed at a private dinner where Camus' blends were presented that he was "a god of the sake industry."

We at Sake Today were admittedly skeptical at first when Heavensake hit the market. The name was playful to the point of riskiness — was this just gimmicky marketing? When we realized which breweries Heavensake was working with to produce the blends, however, we were encouraged to try it. We appreciated the fact, too, that they weren't hiding the producers of the sake, like many private label brands do (often stipulated by the breweries). Furthermore, the price points seemed quite reasonable. We would almost say affordable given the breweries involved, the quality of the sake, and the classiness of the bottles. Heavensake wasn't simply a ploy to appeal to luxury markets or tastes with a mark-up. Heavensake gave every impression of having enough lift-off to reach the mainstream.

So far, consumers seem to agree. Quality doesn't always sell on its own, however, especially in a market crowded with so many brands. Heavensake has benefitted from very intentional and savvy marketing, owing to the "three friends" that conceived it and who, given their backgrounds, understand this aspect of the project about as well as anyone in the world could.

Hirschmann provides some piercing insights from the history of rosé wine. He notes how the producers in France began collaborating with each other to conquer the American market, understanding how combined power would be necessary. They then thought carefully about projecting a certain image, which included steps like packaging their wine in big magnum bottles so that people would associate it with celebration. Gradually, rosé began eating market share from champagne.

Hirschmann says, "Rosé became a party drink at the beach. It's about coolness, the emotional connection you make. People remembered dancing on a table in the summer with their rosé. The shift in perception changed when people started drinking it at fun times."

Do any search of Heavensake images on the internet and you'll see that they're taking this approach. Images abound of celebrities and socialites partying with the bottles. Even if you don't relate to those worlds, nobody should underestimate the power of what can happen when a product breaks into the realm of popular consciousness because of a celebrity or socialite.

Hirschmann notes that sake could be on the cusp of something like this on a smaller scale. Already celebrities like actress Cate Blanchett and musician/producer Pharrell are dabbling with sake brands. Brooklyn Beckham has a sake brand. David Grohl collaborated with Tatenokawa, even. Meanwhile, the sake industry mostly seems interested in appealing to celebrity chefs and influencers in the culinary world. Hirschmann acknowledges that chefs and sommeliers are an important pillar of the industry, but that they are confined to a very specific demographic.

Or, in his words, "Their trend potential is confined to their ecosystem. When it comes to making an alcohol product break into mainstream consumption, the amount of trend-setting power and influence must come from way beyond that microcosm."

It becomes a question of celebrity power, he explains. This is a very special button that needs to be pressed smartly and with the right timing.

"I don't think the category is quite there yet," he admits, "but I travel a lot and I feel a shift."

Whether sake reaches that level of the stratosphere through Heavensake's lead remains to be seen. But those of us who have already discovered sake can enjoy the fruits of Heavensake's line-up regardless. The core products are all junmai sake because that's what the founders swear by personally, though they are going to bend the rules slightly for a limited edition collaboration with Naohiko Noguchi, an industry legend who refuses to retire at age ninety. The reasoning here is that other styles are a part of his legacy.

Camus, meanwhile, is not likely to be found at da club, sipping sake like it's your birthday (though who knows?). When asked how he prefers to enjoy his sake, besides in a large glass, he mentions seafood, specifically large prawns as a pleasing pairing option. He also favours Roquefort cheese.

Wherever Camus goes, however, the reception is one of welcome. Breweries are kind and receive them with that special Japanese hospitality. Many of them, of course, have wondered what assemblage is all about. Imagine if you lived in the Champagne region and a bunch of Japanese folks showed up and said, "Look, we're going to play around with your products and you're going to see — it's going to be interesting." But the look on their faces when they sip his magic is, by all accounts, one of surprise and delight.

Will it be the same for you? For the next celebrity to get a hold of a bottle? For the chefs of the world or the average consumer? That would be good for all of us invested in this industry, emotionally or otherwise. It would be, well, heavenly...
