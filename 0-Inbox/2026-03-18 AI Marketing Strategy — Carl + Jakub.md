## Carl's Philosophy & Thinking

Carl's opening framing — honest and unfiltered assessment of the AI disruption:

> "My personal opinion, I am absolutely obsessed with AI and I think that you are probably well informed about the technological advancements. The game is changing and I don't know what your personal strategy is to adapt to these advancements and where they are today and also where they will be in six months or a year from now. I think for a lot of players in this industry it's going to create a bit of a problem."

On the commoditization of agency services — he's watching Meta do it:

> "Facebook is already creating an infrastructure to make it easy for companies to buy more ads and not have to be dependent on intermediaries."

His prediction for Jakub's clients within 12 months:

> "A year from now, bro, I don't know how much of your clock clients you may want to keep the clients but — if a client gives you 2000 a month now it's going to go down to 500 and you're gonna. The 30% of the clients are going to disappear and the ones that are going to remain are going to reduce their workload. It's just the name of the game."

Carl's vision for what survives the disruption — hybrid humans:

> "Maybe finding ways of people to become more of hybrids where they sort of help the companies implement the tools that technically replace some of the things they were offering before but at the same time create also new opportunities to offer just a different service basically."

On what HEAVENSAKE needs — a precise profile:

> "What I need is a 25 year old guy that is tech savvy but not a programmer. He also understands that it's probably not gonna be a very successful thing unless you already have the relationships… If you're a 25 year old guy now and you see this opportunity and you also understand you have to learn the tools. Like it's not just about saying oh cool story. It's really being like bro — if you do not understand how to interact, it's the same as not knowing how to use email."

On AI performance vs human performance — his framing is confrontational and clarifying:

> "If you tell me an LLM is only at 80% there — which is true in certain cases — what do you think a human being is? What do you think your perfection level is? If you're at 60 or 70, you're already a top performing human being. Nobody is 100."

On the Perplexity restaurant outreach demonstration (he tells Jakub about it):

> "I took our existing account list of restaurants, I gave it to Perplexity computer and said, this is our existing account list in New York. Find me similar restaurants. The thing not only found the restaurants, it found the email and the name of not only one but three different contact people, their LinkedIn. And it can write personalized emails by going and checking every single contact's LinkedIn, Instagram, if they give an interview into a magazine — 'hey, I read your article in Forbes, congratulations.' The opening of the other f***ing thing, it's insane. It's absolutely insane."

On the value of internal AI knowledge:

> "It's also interesting to internally have the knowledge otherwise you constantly sort of depend on people's reports and even the reports depend on how you interpret the metrics. Human beings are just not perfect. So if you tell me an LLM is only 80% perfect — yeah — but what do you think a human being is?"

On the Entropic marketing case — one person running a billion-dollar company's marketing:

> "I don't know if you saw the marketing of Entropic was made by one guy. One guy. So it's. The game is changing and I need people that are really fascinated by the subject and that want to have an opportunity to be part of a cool brand."

On what he wants from the working relationship:

> "I want to work with people that understand that and share that in a way that we can be like, okay, you know, let's be honest with each other. You're also allowed to confront me if you think, look, this is delusional, but I can show you the examples that I base myself on."

His customer activation thesis (delivered to Jakub):

> "Right now what I need is to set up an entire automation system or customer management system to be able to figure out of these 5,000 contacts, which ones are the thousand. Then the, out of the thousand, which ones are the top 20%, which are usually the ones that create the most value. So you're down to about 200 and then to say, well how do you manage these 200, how do you unlock the most value from them? Because these 200 people, I know for sure that we can get between 1,000 to $2,000 a year in sales out of them. That's 200 to $400,000 in sales just with managing these people well."

## Key Strategic Decisions

### Jakub's Role Assessment
- Jakub's three service areas: (1) content automation (SEO, social) (2) paid ads (3) B2B lead generation
- Carl's view: content handled, GEO handled, **paid ads is the strongest potential play**
- Carl does not want to pay €100/hour (~€800/day, €16K/month) — "makes no sense for us"
- Jakub's proposed model: outcome-based fee (% of revenue generated) — Carl likes this in principle

### Paid Ads Position
- Carl acknowledges AI isn't 100% on paid ads yet — still needs "hand holding"
- Jakub: "I don't think AI can generate a video of you going to a nice restaurant and the experience"
- Jakub proposes: AI for targeting, copy, reporting; human oversight for budget/strategy
- Carl wants paid ads tested on the existing database first, not cold acquisition

### GEO Status
- Went from low single-digit to low double-digit LLM mention percentage
- 2 months in, tracking across 6 major LLMs

### What HEAVENSAKE Actually Needs Right Now
- Set up CRM automation to segment 5,000 contacts → identify 1,000 real prospects → focus on top 200
- $200–$400K potential revenue from top 200 contacts with proper management
- Before any cold ad spend, fix the inner circle

### Hiring Profile
- **Not** a programmer, not a traditional ad buyer
- Age 25-ish, tech-savvy, AI-fluent, curious about the brand, hungry
- Works like a hybrid: implements AI tools + provides strategic judgment
- Outcome-based compensation preferred

## Brand DNA Insights

- **Speed of AI adoption = strategic advantage:** Carl is explicitly building for a world where HEAVENSAKE operates with a lean, AI-augmented team that can outexecute larger companies. The agency model's death is HEAVENSAKE's opportunity.
- **The 200-person thesis:** Carl's commercial model is bottom-up and relational — find the 200 best customers, serve them extraordinarily well, generate $400K from people who already know and love the brand. This is the foundation before any cold acquisition.
- **Honesty as culture:** Carl explicitly invites Jakub to challenge him. He wants collaborators who will push back, not just execute. This is his stated management philosophy.
- **The Entropic one-person marketing insight:** The benchmark isn't a 10-person agency — it's one brilliant person with AI tools running the entire marketing of a multi-billion dollar company. This is what Carl is optimizing toward.

## Summary

This call is Carl's clearest articulation of the AI disruption thesis directed at a marketing professional. He is not theoretical — he shows Jakub a live demonstration of what Perplexity can do for restaurant lead generation, compares AI accuracy to human accuracy and argues AI wins, and predicts Jakub's clients will reduce or leave within a year.

But the call also reveals Carl's true vision for the HEAVENSAKE marketing setup: not agencies, not employees in the traditional sense, but hybrid humans — people who understand AI tools deeply, have authentic taste and curiosity about the brand, work on outcome-based compensation, and will honestly push back when something is wrong.

The $200–$400K thesis from 200 customers is the operational core: before any cold ad spend, HEAVENSAKE needs to activate what it already has. The CRM/automation infrastructure to do this is the immediate priority, and paid ads against the existing database is where Jakub's skills are most immediately valuable.
