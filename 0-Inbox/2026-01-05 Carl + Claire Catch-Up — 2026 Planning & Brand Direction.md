## Carl's Philosophy & Thinking

Carl's theory of brand management in the AI era, delivered directly to Claire:

> "What companies are going to need is people who are team players, organizers, managers, leaders, coordinators of all these great assets and tools. And it's a bit of a revolution because we have access to all these great knowledge, these great helpers in AI. And so it becomes really about who has the brain capacity to use those tools, organize themselves, know how to leverage them, know how to work together with other people to create momentum."

Diagnosing the content strategy problem bluntly:

> "The most annoying thing is that we do not really have a strategy. We do not have an identity and we don't have a team to create content online. It's always an improvised story. It has a certain level of harmony because it's based on things that come from our network and we approved them accordingly before. And so there's a bit of a harmony but it's not possible to scale the business like that."

On what brand content actually requires — using the Monday video content process as the example of what a real brand manager does:

> "You know, it's a lot of what people misunderstand about marketing in general or management in general is that it's a lot of annoying, uncool, unfun stuff. Even if you know you."

On the frustration of generic AI marketing plans — he asks Gemini to create a LinkedIn strategy and reacts to the output:

> "Okay so it's what annoys me with this situation is like yeah is our strategy and our job to just constantly be like yeah Assemblage and Noguchi and — no, no, we need to change this. We need to change this. This is never going to work. This is never going to resonate with people. To be like, oh let me order a bottle at home. You need, you need genius. You can't. This is boring stuff."

> "It's depressing, that's what it is."

His standard for content: people need to have an involuntary reaction:

> "People need to be like holy, look at this, look at whatever. Like you need, you need genius."

On what LinkedIn thought leadership always reduces to:

> "It just always comes back to — you need to have these leading figures. So either it's a founder it could be Regis in the case of [the sake story]."

On AI avatar for Regis speaking English — Carl is genuinely curious:

> "We could transform Ray Jesus voice into English and create an avatar of him. At least for LinkedIn it could be a fun experience to do."

On content and events being a unified system:

> "At the party in my house, if there was no social media, the value of that party would be 10 times less. It would just be people coming, having a good time and leaving, nobody would ever care or know about it. But through social media it becomes an international sensation. So they are all connected. Everything is one big thing that goes under that brand essence."

On brand manager role and what it actually requires:

> "A brand manager needs to. In the context of a small company, you're not working for a big thing where you have 20 people under you. You need to have your team. You need to know how to execute campaigns. You need to know how to make sure it is integrated into all these different social media platforms… For we're a digital marketing brand."

On 10 years of the brand and the honest reckoning:

> "It has now been 10 years of us positioning this brand as this elegant, more wild, cool party brand, something in between that has great advantages… And yet the growth and the success of this so far has been a bit limited. At least not up to the wishes that I have."

On what you always need:

> "You're still always going to be as good as your last campaign."

On the need for a trusted network of creatives:

> "You need to have a guy that comes back to you in three days with three different options that blow you away. Or you're sitting in your chair thinking when the am I going to be able to publish this thing? Because I can't wait to show the world how sick our content is."

## Brand DNA Insights

- **The brand is 100 little decisions:** Same principle as expressed in March meeting — brand identity is accumulated micro-choices that create harmony. Can't be outsourced to AI without human taste filtering.
- **Generic = death:** The moment content becomes generic (AI LinkedIn plans, assemblage-repeat messaging), it stops working. The brand's strength is its specificity and unusualness.
- **The party → content pipeline:** Events are not ends in themselves. They are content generation machines. The party value = social media reach, not room size.
- **Founder voice is the irreplaceable asset:** Every AI plan Carl evaluates arrives at the same conclusion — the brand needs prominent, authentic human faces (Carl, Regis). This is both a strength (genuinely compelling story) and a vulnerability (personal availability).
- **10 years: honest reckoning:** Carl uses this call to name what hasn't worked — not enough scale, no cohesive content strategy. The 10th anniversary is not just a celebration; it's a reset point.
