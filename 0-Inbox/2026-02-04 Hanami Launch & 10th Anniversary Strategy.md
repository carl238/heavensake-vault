## Carl's Philosophy & Thinking

Carl opens with a strategic pivot framing — the Women's Day bottle cancellation becomes a better opportunity:

> "The news is that just randomly having a sort of a pink bottle for Women's Day is a bit like — it's too random. There's not really a. Even with the woman Toji and everything. It's the whole Woman's Day thing is a very specific thing. And if you don't have a full concept where you say, okay, we also work with a charity and we also do this or do that. Just a product like this. It's just too random."

> "What it then generates as a conclusion is actually a better plan… We're missing our opportunity to use this in Japan for the launch of our E Commerce there, which gives us a little bit more time by making this bottle a Hanami season."

His vision for Hanami's cultural depth:

> "Hanami — highly culturally relevant moment in Japanese. The Japanese year. It's the start of the spring. It's when the cherry blossoms flourish. It's a very positive moment. It's a much more interesting subject matter."

On the e-commerce strategy for luxury sake in Japan — the attract-then-downsell model:

> "What is essential in E commerce in alcohol in general? Luxury. Absolutely essential. Two things. One having limited editions releases that are not available in other platforms so that it makes people go to Europe. And second thing — do this with very expensive products where Japanese have a lot of spending money on very expensive. Our competitor sake 100 — they sell the average bottle around like 350 or whatever. They have waiting lists of 10,000 people."

> "You attract people into your ecosystem and then you downsell or whatever it's called to say — so that's the strategy. When you then sell them a $100 bottle it seems like a good deal."

On keeping Women's Day out of the brand entirely:

> "No, I think we need to stay away from that in general. It's not something that a brand should sort of capitalize on or leverage. Let's stay away."

On why Hanami is part of HEAVENSAKE's DNA, not just a marketing moment:

> "The whole Hanami thing is part of our DNA and we need to celebrate that in a cool way every year. So this is something."

On the 10th anniversary concept — Assemblage as metaphor for the brand's entire life:

> "Let's start with the idea of the 10 anniversary first. So the idea of 10 anniversary first is to elevate the concept of assemblage. So assemblage is not only for sake but is also for other aspects of HEAVENSAKE in the last 10 years."

> "It might be assemblage of liquid, assemblage of place — because we celebrate all over the world: Fred, Monaco, Japan, Europe, Miami. Assemblage of people — it's related with designer, chef, Toji. Assemblage of culture — HEAVENSAKE is contaminated with music, with arts, with design. And the last one is the assemblage of desire — we create something that the customer deserves. They want prestige. We create scarcity. This might be the storytelling for the 10 year anniversary."

On the 10-year anniversary event structure:

> "The 10 year anniversary is in June and it starts in June. There's technically two 10th anniversaries. The one in South France and then one in — I mean we need one in the U.S. I think we need something in the U.S. So it's going to be France, U.S., Japan — June, [September], November."

On Prestige 2 specifics — why it exists:

> "Prestige 1 is with Noguchi, the brewery and Prestige 2 is with Nizawa. And the reason why is that in Japan, Noguchi did not want us to sell Prestige worn in Japan. So we created Prestige 2."

On the bottle design decision — matte vs shiny:

> "The shiny bottle was a bit cheap. You know, it's just like not very interesting. The sanded bottle is really interesting. I think it's more. It has more. And the issue is the grain of the pink."

His email to Matsumoto-san, dictated live:

## Key Strategic Decisions

### Hanami Bottle (Prestige 2)
- **Decision:** Pivot from Women's Day to Hanami season (late March–early April Tokyo: 31 March–3 April 2026)
- This unlocks 2-3 more weeks for production + Japan e-commerce launch planning
- Bottle: sanded/matte finish preferred over shiny (cheap look)
- Print color: match the porcelain tone of the bottle's ceramic base — "harmony between print and raw ceramic"
- Allocation: exploring 50 Japan + 50 US split (100 total if ARITA can produce)

- Specific bracelets designed for the party

## Brand DNA Insights

- **Hanami as brand DNA, not marketing:** Carl is firm that Hanami is not a campaign hook — it's who HEAVENSAKE is. The cherry blossom season is woven into the brand's identity because the brand emerged from Japanese sake culture. Capitalizing on Women's Day would be a posture; celebrating Hanami is authentic.
- **Assemblage transcends sake:** The assemblage concept (blending different rice varieties, breweries, years to create something greater than its parts) applies to the brand's entire biography — its global geographic assemblage, its cultural cross-contamination with music/art/design, its assemblage of desire (scarcity-driven luxury). This is a profound conceptual expansion of the brand's core differentiator.
- **Women's Day: brands shouldn't opportunistically capitalize:** Carl's instinct against Women's Day content is principled — not against the cause but against a brand attaching itself to a culturally heavy day without a full, genuine commitment (charity, sustained work). This reflects his anti-superficiality principle.
- **Attract with luxury, serve with access:** The e-commerce model is funnel-based: use a ¥70,000 limited edition as the entry point/data capture, then serve those people with ¥10,000 bottles. HEAVENSAKE's premium positioning becomes a lead-generation tool.

## Summary

This meeting is where Carl executes one of his characteristic strategic moves: taking a challenge (Women's Day bottle rejected by U.S. PR consultant) and converting it into a better opportunity (Hanami bottle tied to Japan e-commerce launch). The Women's Day rejection is actually liberating — it gives more production time, aligns the bottle with Japan's most culturally resonant seasonal moment, and avoids the brand positioning risk of co-opting a politically charged day.

The deeper strategic insight: the Hanami bottle IS the Japan e-commerce launch. A limited edition (50 bottles at ¥70,000) creates enough premium positioning and exclusivity to drive traffic, capture customer data, and establish HEAVENSAKE as a serious player in the Japanese DTC market. Once someone enters the ecosystem via the premium limited edition, they're naturally served the fuller portfolio.

The 10th anniversary framework around "Assemblage of Everything" is one of Carl's most sophisticated brand communications insights. He takes the technical concept that defines HEAVENSAKE's sake (blending breweries, rice types, vintages) and elevates it into a metaphor for the entire brand biography: a decade of assembling places, people, cultures, and desires into something greater than any single element. This gives the anniversary campaign a conceptual spine that works in all markets and all media.
