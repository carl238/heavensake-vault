# HEAVENSAKE Brand Knowledge Base

This document is a living brand reference compiled from 37 recorded calls between Carl Hirschmann (Founder/CEO) and Claire Senda (Assistant Brand Manager), spanning December 2025 through March 2026. It is organized by theme, not chronology. Every insight has been synthesized and deduplicated — call sources are noted in parentheses for traceability. Use this as your first stop before any brand decision, content brief, or creative direction. When in doubt, ask: *What is the HEAVENSAKE way to do this?*

## Brand Philosophy & Core Identity

### The Soul of HEAVENSAKE

HEAVENSAKE is an East-meets-West brand built on the conviction that sake — one of the world's great traditional drinks — deserves to be positioned as a luxury cultural object, not an ethnic specialty. The brand was founded in 2016 and its 10th anniversary (2026) is a milestone moment across all communications.

> "We're modern, so we're not Japanese. That's the whole point of having sake — we take cool stuff and we take a traditional thing every week and we play around with it. But it also stands for real stuff." (Call 2)

> "A brand is a hundred little things that sort of create a harmony. And you could say that specific one doesn't matter." (Call 8)

The brand operates at three simultaneous levels:
- **Cultural movement** — changing the global perception of sake
- **Luxury product** — among the finest crafted alcoholic beverages in the world
- **Digital-first media brand** — everything generates content and social value

### Core Positioning Principles

- **Luxury AND timeless:** "We are luxury. We are timeless. That is something that we need to always sort of like think about. It's like there's a certain timelessness in what we want to do. Not jump on every trend." (Call 11)
- **Art, not just product:** "It's not about the product, it's about art." (Call 16)
- **Emotion creation:** "We are in the business of creating emotions in people." (Call 11)
- **Japanese timelessness as DNA:** The timeless quality of Japanese culture — not its trends — is the brand's reference. Method and patience, not novelty. (Call 11)
- **Brand = 100 small things:** Extreme attention to detail (spacing, tone, material quality) adds up to brand quality. No single element is trivial. (Call 8)

### What Makes HEAVENSAKE Unique

- **First sake brand with deep music industry access:** "We're literally the first sake brand ever to go and have access to such musicians. And they bring sake on stage and they bring sake onto their podcasts." (Call 16)
- **[[RZA]] moment is historic:** "RZA is forever the first rapper to have brought a bottle of sake on stage." (Call 16)
- **[[Regis Camus]] as the champagne-sake bridge:** No other sake brand can tell the story of the world's most-awarded champagne maker hand-crafting a sake assemblage. (Calls 1, 11, 26)
- **Pioneered sake in celebration contexts:** Proving sake is suitable for parties, stages, and musical moments was something HEAVENSAKE built from scratch. (Call 16)

### Two Pillars of Quality

> "The gold standard is great product, good price, amazing marketing, and your cost of operating your entire infrastructure and creating your content — it does not burn you." (Call 4)

Product quality is the entry ticket:
> "How good is the quality of your product? Okay, that's your opportunity to sit at the table. Because if you try to sell a shit product, even if your marketing is fun and everything — over time, your product is shit. So you're done." (Call 4)

### Brand Knowledge as a System

Carl explicitly designed these calls as a knowledge-building exercise:
> "You and I, all these hours that we are recording, we need to have a bit of a tracking about them because all this knowledge, all this thinking, all these insights and all this thing that I'm sharing with you, they're supposed to actually become sort of, you know, a compass, a system also for yourself... Nothing is random." (Call 11)

### Brand Over Sales Mentality

HEAVENSAKE prioritizes brand equity over transactional revenue in every collaboration and campaign:

> "It's about the brand. It's not about selling a bottle. It's about what the brand stands for, it's about what we put out there." (Call 25)

> "As soon as you link it to the sale of even one bottle, you are losing the beauty because you're trying to sell bottles." (Call 25)

> "We are in the business of creating emotions in people." (Call 11)

### Digital-First Thinking

Everything HEAVENSAKE does — events, collaborations, products — must translate into digital content and social value:

> "We're a digital marketing brand... Even when we do events, the goal is for that to translate into digital media content." (Call 26)

> "If there was no social media, the value of that party would be 10 times less." (Call 26)

### The "Heaven Sake Way" Test

Before executing any campaign, activation, or event, ask: *Is this the Heaven Sake way to do it?*

> "Having a little Heaven Sake stand somewhere with us standing behind it and cheering people with our little sake glass and be like, hey, you want to taste a little — here's a little plastic cup for you to have a little sip is not a Heaven Sake way to do it." (Call 19)

The standard: Cool, luxury, playful, creates virality, makes people want to buy, generates great content, doesn't cost a fortune.

### The Name as a Design Principle

The word "Heaven" is not just a name — it is a positioning principle:
> "You should always start putting it as high as possible because it is heaven." (Call 31)

Brand elements placed high in compositions reinforce this. "PURE ELEVATION" (the tagline) should always be in ALL CAPS.

### What the Brand is NOT

- Not a streetwear/hype culture fashion brand
- Not a pre-packaged cocktail brand (doing that risks sliding toward Campari territory)
- Not a brand that uses artists as marketing tools — see [[Partnerships & Collaborations]]
- Not a trend-follower: "Not jump on every trend. Content wise, graphic design wise." (Call 11)
- Not generic: Never participate in cultural moments (Women's Day, Valentine's Day) without a deeply meaningful, brand-authentic story. Generic participation is worse than silence. (Call 10)

## The Execution Thesis

Carl's assessment as of March 2026: HEAVENSAKE has all the ingredients for success. What remains is systems and execution.

> The brand is really, really strong and desired. It's just a question of execution. We have: the product, the portfolio with a nice price range from $35 to $700, the brand credibility, the distribution, the history, the storytelling, the great design. We have everything it takes. Now it's just about systems and execution.

This is not a brand problem or a product problem — it is an infrastructure and cadence problem. The solution is AI-driven systems that enable the required consistency and output without proportional headcount growth.

### Prestige 3 — Sakura Pink Bottle

- **Material:** Arita Yaki porcelain produced by [[Matsumoto-san]] at Arita Porcelain Lab
- **Finish:** Sanded matte pink — "It's not just pink, it's also like a sanded pink. So it's much softer to the touch. It has like — it's like really, really, really sexy." (Call 20)
- **Production process:** Bottle dipped head-first (upside down) into pink glaze, dried, then fired in kiln at 2,000–3,000°C. The process is applied by hand by a female artisan. (Calls 13, 14)
- **Distribution:** 120 bottles ordered from Arita Lab — 2 empty for decoration, 59 to Noguchi brewery, 59 to Nizawa brewery. (Call 14)
- **Kanji:** Carl strongly prefers the simplified three horizontal lines (三) — "For me, it's really heaven, actually." Symbolizes earth, sky, and what comes above. (Call 11)
- **Label regulations:** Any text change triggers new US legal documentation; only the color change is planned to avoid this. (Call 20)
- **Launch plan:** Pre-release to 200 "gold members," online launch targeted for June, full launch September. (Call 1)
- **Long-term:** Carl is not fully settled on whether pink stays permanent or Prestige 3 eventually transitions to the same color as Prestige 1 and 2. (Call 8)

### Prestige Porcelain Production Standards

- The Arita sandblasting ("suna fuki") process justifies a cost of ~€10 per bottle — a meaningful artisanal process, not a gimmick. (Call 10)
- 51 bottles ordered per brewery (1 kept as empty display); 60 bottles per brewery for standard runs. (Calls 1, 10)
- Professional product photography is non-negotiable for this price point: "This is an €800 bottle. Why are you — a professional photographer for €480 for three really high quality photos from a good angle is absolutely a good deal." (Call 13)
- AI photos will not replace professional photos: "An AI photo is just never going to be the same as a real photo." (Call 13)

### Cocktail Philosophy

- **Azure is the designated cocktail sake.** Noir is for artistic/display settings. (Call 28)
- **No other alcohol in cocktails, ever.** Sake + tea (green tea preferred — more Japanese, stronger visual) only. No Schweppes, no sugary sodas. (Call 28)
- **One masterpiece over three mediocre cocktails:** "I want something that I want to do at my house. I want something that I want to serve in Monaco during the party." (Call 28)
- Green tea is preferred over black tea for cocktails. (Call 28)
- The best cocktail should aspire to enter the "official HEAVENSAKE cocktail list." (Call 28)
- Visual standard: no lemon slices ("so 90s"), not "depressing" in a glass. (Call 28)
- Tea Bucks collaboration cocktail is a candidate for the official list. (Call 28)

### Product Development Philosophy

- Never dilute the core offering: "I'm not gonna launch a new drink... We're not going to start having a cocktail." (Call 24)
- Focus stays on the existing portfolio.
- Scarcity and restraint are luxury markers: offering 3–10 of something (never mass quantities) reinforces exclusivity. (Calls 22, 23)

### Key Product Stories for Storytelling

**Shichigen Water Journey (15 years):**
> "The water used for Shichigen sake... the water that arrives took something like 15 years to travel down." It travels through geological strata — rocks, layers of earth — before arriving. (Call 37)
This is a cinematic storytelling asset: patience, purity, and time embodied.

**Arita Yaki Process:**
The bottle dipping process is brand storytelling gold. The kiln interior at 2,000–3,000°C can only be visualized through AI — a unique content opportunity. (Call 14)

**Sake-Calligraphy Connection:**
Both water (essential to ink and to sake), milling/polishing (rice polishing mirrors ink grinding), and patience connect calligraphy and sake-making at a philosophical level. "The black heaven sake bottle could be like a drop of ink." (Call 27)

### Event Bottle Ratios (Operational Reference)

- Art gallery cocktail reception (~100 guests, sole alcohol): ~30 bottles
- Closing event (~60 guests): ~24 bottles; 60 sake glasses + 60 water glasses
- Restaurant dinner (28 guests): ~1 bottle per 5 guests → 6 Azure, 6 Noir, 6 Orange, 4 Prestige
- General rule: half a bottle per guest in a party setting; less in reception settings
- Emergency stash: keep 6 bottles on hand at base (hotel, office) (Call 29)

## Visual Identity & Design System

### Core Visual DNA

Four words define HEAVENSAKE's visual identity:
> "Light, elevation, purity, minimalism. Light. We've always been about light. It's always been about light." (Call 26)

### Breathing Space as Luxury Marker

> "I like when things breathe and it's one of the luxury things. Anytime you have the opportunity to make something breathe or more spacey." (Call 8)

White space is not empty — it is a signal of luxury. Every layout, T-shirt design, carousel, and print piece must give elements room to breathe.

### Logo & Wordmark Rules

- **The wordmark must always appear.** The hexagon alone is not enough: "There's no Heaven Sake anywhere. Where's Heaven Sake?" (Call 16) The brand recognition is still being built — every visual touchpoint must include the legible name.
- **No design decisions without Carl.** Particularly for the website. (Call 16)
- **Generally right-bound alignment** when text must choose left or right. (Call 11)
- **Brand elements placed high:** "You should always start putting it as high as possible because it is heaven." (Call 31)
- **Rule of thirds for product imagery:** Bottle centered at the intersection point; logo/wordmark in the upper portion; hexagon centered cleanly. (Call 33)
- **Cover photo horizon line:** Must bisect the negative space — not cut through or touch the hexagon. The hexagon floats cleanly above the horizon. (Call 32)
- **One line is always cleaner than two:** "Two lines is always too much. Like why two lines? One you see like one line." (Call 11)
- **"PURE ELEVATION" in ALL CAPS** for the tagline wherever used. (Call 33)

### The Hexagon

- Use the hexagon with **restraint** — not on every post. "In general, I think I want to use the hexagon less." (Call 11)
- "Every post has that hexagon, it becomes too much." (Call 16)
- Sometimes less is more: "I just think it's cleaner." (Call 16)
- In carousels, the hexagon creates visual rhythm only when used selectively. (Call 16)
- On Shikishi message cards: hexagon logo in UV acrylic print — proportions must be right. (Calls 25, 27)

### Color Philosophy

- The Brand Bible colors are not fixed canon: "It wasn't that precise. It was their suggestion... The Brand Bible needs a bit of love." (Call 11) Treat it as a base to refine, not law.
- **Label Noir = all black aesthetic:** The butterfly must be black. Tone-on-tone. No surprising accent colors breaking the noir theme. (Call 15)
- **Red is off-brand:** "Heaven Sake is not very red vibes." (Call 11) Red accents from the Rolling Stones logo were rejected.
- **Avoid "flat" colors:** Carl rejected a pink Prestige printing sample because "the pink is a little bit too flat." (Call 1)
- **Prestige 3 = matte sanded pink:** This informs all associated content's color direction. (Call 20)
- **Soft backgrounds:** "I think we need the background color to be a little bit more soft." (Call 9)

### Typography

- **White text with black outlines** on certain print/social materials (e.g., the Rolling Stones carousel) — the existing font gets an outline layer added, not a new font. (Call 7)
- **Kanji spacing:** Elements should be slightly closer together; avoid too much spacing. (Call 9)
- **Prestige 3 kanji:** A more modern simplified kanji was chosen — "I want Prestige 3 to be a bit more modern. The simple F looks absolutely amazing." (Call 8)
- **Center-chest logo placement is rejected as a trend**, not timeless. (Call 11)

### Business Cards

- **ALL HEAVENSAKE business cards are vertical.** Non-negotiable. "All Evansaki business cards are vertical. You need to stick to the base design... It's a vertical with Heaven Sake [logo] at the top part." (Call 22)
- Japanese on the back, English on the front.
- Paper choice should match the quality reference established by Tsuruta-san. (Call 22)

### Design Approval Principle

Storytelling determines whether a design is approved or rejected:
> "The difference between the design being approved and the design being random is your storytelling. Because before I was like, yeah, okay, cute. But what — I mean, how does that — and so the explanation really changes the... And if your story adds up, it becomes poetry, it becomes art." (Call 11)

Every design choice must be answerable: *Why is this green? Why is this like this? Why did you choose this?*

### Feed Aesthetics (Instagram)

- **Instagram is the #1 brand window.** "The Instagram needs to be the perfect, perfect window." (Call 7) It is seen by tens of thousands; a physical object is seen by hundreds.
- **Cover/hero images** must be harmonized with the feed — background softness is essential. (Calls 7, 9)
- **Soft edges concept from website:** Straight angles at the bottom, soft edges at the top. (Call 9)
- **Do not post two carousels back to back.** (Call 16)
- **Balance product types:** "There's all these prestige content is a little bit intense — it's all prestige. So that's why the black bottle becomes really important to post." (Call 16)

### Thumbnail Thinking

Every video must be designed to stand out at 1/9th of a screen size, with zero sound, in a fraction of a second:
> "You don't even have two seconds. You have a quarter of a second in a ninth of the screen... you need something that if it's just light..." (Call 26)

### Daruma Visual Direction

- **Face:** Traditional Japanese phoenix and dragon design (not modern cute/minimal or heron variants). "That's the traditional way of doing the dragon and the phoenix. And I want this, the eye to be exactly like this. Clean." (Call 2)
- **Phoenix is personal to Carl:** "I prefer the phoenix because I have a personal relationship with the phoenix." (Call 8)
- **Paint style:** Hand-painted appearance where "some parts are lighter than the other" — not washi printing. (Call 2)
- **Eye shape:** Brushstroke shape, slightly rectangular — not a perfect circle. (Call 1)
- **Pre-printed vs. hand-painted:** Base face (phoenix/dragon) and kanji arrive pre-printed. [[Mommy (Mami San)]] paints only the second eye and the luck kanji on the back. (Calls 2, 8)
- **10th Anniversary Daruma:** HEAVENSAKE photos from the brand's history are printed on washi paper and adhered to the Daruma surface; Mami San then paints over/around them. Size: 70–76cm, white/unpainted base. (Call 37)
- **The Daruma is kept after completion** — not burned. Culturally important. (Call 26)

### Materials Quality Standard

- **No polyester or synthetic materials** — for scrolls, merchandise, or art objects. "I don't want polyester anyway." Silk is acceptable. Authentic, natural materials only. (Call 33)
- **Scrolls:** Regional provenance matters — Kyoto (silk) acceptable; Harajuku (polyester) rejected. Budget: 80,000 yen + calligraphy fee. (Call 33)

### Video Aesthetic: Founders Interview Series

- Visual reference: Stanley Kubrick's *2001: A Space Odyssey*
- Setting: White space, directional lighting creating a triangle of darkness
- Setup: Two subjects (Sarah interviewing Etienne/Carl) in kimonos, side-profile, tea-ceremony-style table with reflective surface
- [[Etienne]]'s lighting team is essential — "Etienne is the king of light." (Call 26)

### T-Shirt Design Principles

- **Weight:** 180–190 GSM preferred — the lowest end of good quality. Carl dislikes thick streetwear-style. (Call 1)
- **The white sweater is a permanent, settled SKU.** "The white sweater will exist for as long as Evansake exists because it is proven, it is tested." Never revisit this decision. (Call 1)
- **Wearability = marketing ROI:** "The difference between you burning money on something stupid and you actually generating free marketing value is whether people are going to wear it or not." (Call 1)
- **Embroidery only for simple/thick-line elements** — complex hexagonal/fine-line designs cannot be embroidered. Use silkscreen for those. (Call 8)
- **Never put just a plain logo on a T-shirt** — "it's cheap." The sweater vibe is required. "You need the sweater vibe. Otherwise it's cheap. Just a logo like this on a T shirt, it's cheap." (Call 24)
- **No center-chest placement** — a trend, not timeless. (Call 11)
- **10th Anniversary shirt back design:** Large back print, breathing space between "HEAVENSAKE" wordmark and the mountain/Torii gate design; dates integrated into the design's crevices, not beneath it. Do NOT add "10 years" text. (Calls 8, 11, 25)
- **New bottle illustration shirt:** HEAVENSAKE bottle on black or dark gray ("a bit grayish") — logo with hexagon upper area, kanji somewhere (not split). (Call 25)
- **Production split:** ~200–300 units Japan, ~200–300 Italy/Europe. Sweater versions also. (Call 25)

## Tone of Voice & Messaging

### The Core Principle: Explanation IS the Content

> "The piece of content is not the picture. The piece of content is your explanation about the picture. That's what makes people understand the context of the picture and certainly makes the picture... more interesting." (Call 26)

The why behind a moment is more powerful than the moment itself. Context transforms documentation into storytelling.

### Storytelling as the Master Key

> "If your story adds up, it becomes poetry, it becomes art, and you, it changes everything. So this is what brand world is." (Call 11)

Storytelling is what transforms luxury marketing from nice-to-look-at into culturally meaningful. The why behind design choices must always be answerable.

> "It all came from the storytelling, which is one of the keys of luxury marketing." (Call 11)

### Brand Voice Principles

- **Honor and respect** are central, especially in Japanese contexts: "Especially in Japan, it's a lot about honor. An honor to be welcomed at Academy Divin." (Call 10)
- **Emotional and narrative, not bland:** Captions must tell stories, not describe what's visible.
- **Depth and meaning over surface engagement:** "Either you do something really, really meaningful with depth, with a message, with meaning or you just…" (Call 10)
- **Tag everyone possible** in posts — this is a courtesy and a discovery mechanic. (Call 10)
- **Precision in language:** Every phrase must carry meaning and can be explained. Vague catchphrases are challenged. (Call 33)

### Two-Type Reader Optimization

> "You essentially have two, three different kinds of people that are viewing your content. There's the ones that go through every single image on that carousel and read the full caption. Then there is the category of the people that fly over it. So you need to optimize your content for both technically. Meaning the first two, three lines of the caption need to be quite powerful and efficient... while at the same time you need to add enough substance." (Call 16)

Optimize for skimmers (punchy opening lines) and readers (substantive full caption). Both must be served.

### Caption Direction Examples

**Noguchi carousel:** "Five in the morning snow. Ishikawa. The master starts his day something like this." — Use specific time, place, and atmospheric detail. (Call 9)

**Rolling Stones:** Must mention specific names (Anima Riza, ASAP, Nas, Virgil Abloh, Black Coffee, Solomon, RZA) and the specific story of each. Give context — why a rapper going on stage with sake matters. (Call 16)

**Academy Divin:** "An honor to be welcomed at Academy Divin, Japan's wine school. The fully booked event attended by such a high percentage of women." (Call 10)

**10th Anniversary:** Not the picture itself but Carl's narrated explanation of each of the 10 iconic photos is what makes it powerful. (Call 26)

### SEO-Optimized Captions

Captions must be SEO-optimized to align with the post's subject. This is content infrastructure, not just description. (Call 16)

### What NOT to Do in Messaging

- **No generic cultural moment participation.** Women's Day is about the historical oppression and fight of women — a luxury sake brand cannot participate authentically without genuine conceptual depth. "Please remove this and don't do this again to me please." (Call 10)
- **No "lovey-lovey" Valentine's messaging.** HEAVENSAKE celebrates love for art, for craft, for something that sometimes transcends human love — not romantic clichés. (Call 20)
- **No nihilistic messaging** in campaigns meant to celebrate: "Let's not go down there. Everything ends." — context matters. (Call 22)
- **Personalization must be real.** "The word is personal. If we define what is written, it's not personalized." (Call 24)
- **No vague phrases** that can't be justified. (Call 33)

### Thank You Card Approved Language

> "We are honored to share the singular expressions of Mommy's vision with you. A preserved piece of a human life captured in a single, unrepeatable moment. We thank you for joining us in the celebration of beauty and craft." (Call 22, refined toward "the celebrated beauty of Japanese culture or artisanal artists.")

### On Hyping Yourself Tastefully

> "Finding ways to hype yourself in a way that is elegant and cool and not like — it's actually not that easy and there's not that many opportunities. So this is a very, very good opportunity to put in perspective what it is that we're doing, why we're doing it and why it matters." (Call 16)

HEAVENSAKE should own its achievements without boasting. The music relationships, the Rolling Stones award, the pioneering of sake in celebration contexts — these deserve to be hyped, tastefully.

## Marketing & Advertising Strategy

### Overall Vision

- Goal: Fully AI-optimized marketing operation by September 2026. (Call 4)
- Online sales targets: $3M total ($1M Japan, $1.5–2M US). [[Shichiken]] already does $1M in Japan online sales as a benchmark. (Call 4)
- Previous ad spend of ~$20K/month was burning with no return. Target: $3,400 ad spend → $30,000–40,000 in sales. (Call 4)

### Facebook / Meta Advertising

**Two-campaign sequence:** Engagement/awareness first → conversion second. Never simultaneously. (Calls 1, 2, 6)

**Targeting parameters:**
- Household income, net worth, aspirational high income
- Food interests, wine interests (Wine Spectator, Robert Parker, Napa Valley, Bordeaux, champagne)
- Champagne is an essential targeting interest because of the [[Regis Camus]] storytelling bridge (Call 1)
- Exclude 65+ demographic (Call 1)
- Avoid Facebook Advantage+ placement (prefer manual for control) (Calls 1, 2)

**US geographic focus:**
- Primary: New York, Los Angeles, San Francisco, Miami, Austin
- Secondary: Cities with health-conscious, refined, trend-aware demographics
- Target communities: Japanese, Asian, foodie, tech (Austin), affluent (Miami)
- California insight: People prefer ordering online due to geographic distances. (Call 1)

**Technical requirements:**
- Fix browser-only pixel → add server-side Conversion API tracking. Estimated 16% cost reduction. (Call 5)
- Shopify purchase and email data must be properly connected for retargeting. 121,000 engaged users available. (Call 5)
- Video/animation outperforms static consistently. (Call 5)
- Quality engagement metric for ads: "maximize through plays" (full watch) over "maximize engagement" (includes cheap 3-second replays). (Call 6)

**Facebook ad account history:** Heavensake 2.0 was restricted by Meta. Heavensake 3.0 is active with opportunity score of 103. (Call 6)

**Specialist required:** Paid social is not trial-and-error territory. [[Jakub Kambor]] is the preferred specialist — re-engage him. (Call 34)

**Benchmark:** ~50,000–60,000 views per $300 boost spend. Substantially below this = targeting or setup problem. (Call 34)

**Carousel format is an approved ad unit.** Monitor performance closely — ads should not run passively without tracking. (Call 31)

### Content-First Marketing Philosophy

Content is the primary marketing vehicle. Ads amplify content; they do not replace it.

> "We just need to find them and we need to show them how cool we are. We need to do cool and I'm confident that we're going to do cool shit." (Call 4)

### Organic vs. Paid Balance

A brand needs organic attraction — otherwise it is not a real brand. The organic pull is the proof that the brand resonates.

At the same time, as an alcohol brand, HEAVENSAKE faces significant restrictions on Facebook and Instagram that do not allow the same kind of organic engagement and growth as non-regulated products. The strategy must account for this:

- **Organic:** Proves brand pull, builds community, creates content library, earns credibility
- **Paid:** Required for e-commerce growth, strategic ad spend with precision targeting (see [[Meta Ads Knowledge Base]])
- **Balance:** Both must coexist. Over-reliance on paid means the brand is buying attention, not earning it. Zero paid means leaving e-commerce growth on the table.

The goal is AI-powered systems that maintain the required posting cadence and consistency — the bottleneck is not creative quality but operational throughput.

## Partnerships & Collaborations

### Core Philosophy: Celebrate, Don't Use

> "What we do is we celebrate the artist. We celebrate their work... You're not celebrating the artist when you do this. You are just buying someone to do the promotion of your brand, which is fair enough, but that's not very interesting to the consumer." (Call 28)

The contrast case: Patron Tequila telling Mami San to paint their logo. HEAVENSAKE invites the artist's authentic creative expression. The result should feel like co-creation.

> "The scroll is something where it's a symbol for our relationship. We create something beautiful together." (Call 33)

### Brand as Community Curator

> "It is a way for us to give our community access to unique artists and content from Japan." (Call 23)

HEAVENSAKE's value in collaborations is curation and access — not product promotion. The brand facilitates connections without taking a cut.

> "We are not going to make any money on the actual sale of the artwork. It's purely to support a young artist and to show our dedication to help celebrate Japanese culture." (Call 23)

### How to Approach Partners

- **Always contact the marketing department, not sales.** "Sales only cares about revenue; marketing cares about brand content and exposure." (Call 15)
- **Offer content creation as the value exchange:** "We want to create a beautiful piece of content around [venue] and we want to celebrate [them]." (Call 15)
- **Leverage the Instagram brand value:** "90% of the people have ever asked this look at our stuff and they go like, oh my God, I would love a video like this." (Call 15)
- **Escalate to the owner if the team is unresponsive.** (Call 15)
- **Always reach out from the official HEAVENSAKE account:** "Reach out from the Heavensake account. It's another person that gets to discover the brand." (Call 1)
- **Personal and direct outreach:** "Hey, what up, brother? This looks really cool. I love your work." (Call 1)

### Core Principle: Associate with Great People

> "That's the nature of our business. We try to associate with great people and make them their networks discover Japanese sake." (Call 32)

Every partnership must give **network access** — reaching the partner's audience of quality people. This is the core KPI.

### Tier Decision Is Always Carl's Call

No partner should be assigned a tier without Carl's approval. The framework is a proposal; execution requires founder sign-off. (Call 32)

### Payment Ethics

Carl is firm: designers and artists must be paid for work even if the project is ultimately unused:
> "We will pay because he did the effort and that's it... out of principle we will pay for his time because we respect him and his work a lot." (Call 10)

### No Hidden Fees

Fees should be bundled, not itemized with hidden add-ons. Administrative fees, communication fees, and small extras are unacceptable if added on top of an agreed creative fee. "She inflated the prices of everything by 100%... all inclusive." (Call 37)

## Operational Standards & Working Principles

### Written Confirmation Before Action

> "I need these things in writing and I should have them already today." (Call 33)

Important operational and legal decisions require written confirmation from all parties. Verbal commitments from vendors are insufficient. This applies especially to web infrastructure, contracts, and financial agreements.

### Clarity Before Research

> "You and I need to agree exactly on what it is that you're looking for. What is the budget? What is the profile? Because I can't have you tell me, well, you know, I sort of spend a day reviewing all this stuff." (Call 33)

Before any research or sourcing task: agree on (1) exact deliverable, (2) budget range, (3) specification/profile.

### Quality Standards

> "When I do a video editing I go frame by frame. I give the feedback to the guy frame by frame." (Call 14)

> "The difference between a postable video that is a luxury brand asset and a potentially sort of weird looking sort of improvised little stitched together little thing is massive." (Call 14)

"The difference between 80% and 100% is massive for a luxury brand." The last 20% is everything.

### Close Items Before Opening New Ones

> "You love opening stuff and have these great ideas, but it's all about the actual closing. If you are just throwing a nice little things, you have all your things, then it becomes overwhelming." (Call 13)

Always identify what is "must" vs. "nice to have" before committing resources. (Call 18)

### Vendor Management Framework

- **Never scale a vendor before they've proven themselves on small tasks.** Test bit by bit. (Call 28)
- **Vendors must justify pricing deviations from market rates.** (Call 19)
- **Red flag:** Vendors who disappear when asked to justify quotes. (Call 19)
- **Give honest feedback.** "Give the people honest feedback." (Call 11)
- **Watch out for "pro hustlers"** who use AI to sound smarter in meetings. (Call 28)

### Budget Philosophy

- **Fair pricing explained, not demanded:** "If somebody quotes me 500, another one 2000, I need to understand the technical differences to make a decision." (Call 19)
- **Reward creative initiative:** "If you have five, six of those every two months, because where you come up with cool shit... you could charge $300 of creative and design fee or concept fee on top of what you're doing." (Call 11)
- **AI video production benchmark:** $2,000–$3,000 for cinematic quality. Anything more requires strong justification. (Call 37)
- **Professional photography minimum:** ~€160–200 per product shot; €480 for three shots is a good deal for an €800 product. (Call 13)

Salary and title are tied to demonstrated output quality, speed, and problem-solving — not tenure or hours worked.
