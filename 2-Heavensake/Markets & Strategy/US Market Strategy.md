# US Market Strategy

The US is HEAVENSAKE's largest revenue market ($1.5–2M target for 2026) and its most operationally complex. The 2026 strategy is built on Miami as the laboratory, the 10th anniversary as the national narrative catalyst, and DTC + premium on-premise as the primary channels.

---

## 2024–2025 Performance Reality

The US market underperformed significantly:
- **2024:** Revenue down 40% vs. budget
- **Q1 2025:** $324K loss
- **Florida and Texas** showed promise; New York was the highest-cost, underperforming market
- **Southern Glazers distributor issues:** Technical ordering system problems, chargeback and sample cost escalation, poor ROI on investment into national distribution

Carl's diagnosis: the brand needs to **create consumer demand directly** to pull through distributors — not the traditional push model where the distributor drives sell-through.

## By-the-Glass Strategy

The by-the-glass placement is HEAVENSAKE's primary on-premise growth engine. Key mechanics:

- **Price point resistance:** $22–$28 by-the-glass — market-appropriate but requires active justification
- **Pour size education:** HEAVENSAKE bottles are 720ml (24oz), yielding 6–8 pours. Steer accounts toward 3–4oz pours (not 5oz like wine) → better economics for the account
- **Staff incentivization:** Bottles to take home, gift cards. "These guys aren't making insane amounts of money, they just want to feel supported." Staff who drink HEAVENSAKE sell HEAVENSAKE.
- **Training materials:** Staff training cards, menu copy language, pairing guides, digital marketing support — Zak building a national by-the-glass activation program

## What's NOT Working: The IWA Lesson

HEAVENSAKE is actively studying IWA's approach in the US as a case study in what to avoid:

> IWA is running heavy activation, seeking Michelin positioning, broad fine dining outreach — but not seeing repeat orders.

Their strategy: "get it everywhere and orders will come" — Carl's team sees this as flawed. Premium sake must **earn its place** through staff education, cultural storytelling, and consumer experience, not just distribution breadth.

HEAVENSAKE's counter-approach: concentrate depth in fewer, better accounts rather than spreading thin.

## The Dorsey Program (Under Evaluation)

Dorsey's "Buy the Bottle" program: reservation includes a bottle of HEAVENSAKE as part of the dining experience.

**Pros:** Forces listing at key accounts where HEAVENSAKE struggles to get placement
**Cons:** Dorsey now requires budget commitments (not just free goods), takes a 20% booking fee, and may not be filling seats efficiently in its growth phase

Zak's recommendation: pilot with 3–5 target accounts where HEAVENSAKE has struggled. Carl is deferring to Zak/Nes for fiscal evaluation.
