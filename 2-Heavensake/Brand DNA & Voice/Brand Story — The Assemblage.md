# ASSEMBLAGE — THE ART & SCIENCE

*Source: Assemblage-4.pdf — Technical and conceptual deep dive on the Assemblage technique*

---

## The Concept

The art of champagne-making often involves the skillful blending of different wines to complement one another in a process known as **Assemblage**.

When applied to sake, this technique results in a rounder and more balanced drinking experience reminiscent of those of the best wines. Renowned chef de cave, **Régis Camus**, uses his unique prowess when blending the expressions, combining various sake to bring new flavors and fragrances to the holy Japanese beverage.

Régis likens Assemblage to musical creation, whereby different elements are harmoniously layered to create enchanting compositions. His method ensures the right balance of nose, taste, mouthfeel and length for all products. Through experience, Régis knows which aromas and fragrances merge well to unlock interesting taste profiles.

---

## The Concept

- **Blend sake to create well-balanced compositions**
- **Layer different elements for new aromas, fragrances**
- **Achieve more richness and complexity like great wines**

## Régis Camus' Approach

- Treat sake similarly to champagne
- Categorise each sake by taste profile
- Define a base, add fruity and floral notes, focus on length
- Aim for perfect harmony of nose, taste and mouthfeel
- Process takes three days on average

## ASSEMBLAGE vs BLENDING

### Blending in Traditional Japanese Sake Making

**Definition and Purpose:**
Blending in traditional Japanese sake making refers to the process of combining different batches or tanks of sake to achieve specific goals such as taste uniformity, cost efficiency, and volume management. This practice has been integral in maintaining consistent quality across different batches of sake, especially for larger commercial breweries.

**Historical Context:**

- **Taste Uniformity:** One of the primary reasons for blending in Japanese sake production has been to ensure that the final product has a consistent taste profile. Given the natural variations in fermentation processes and raw materials, blending helps standardize the flavor, aroma, and overall quality of the sake.
- **Cost Savings:** Blending allows breweries to balance higher-quality sake with more economically produced batches, effectively managing costs while still producing a palatable product.
- **Volume Management:** By blending multiple tanks, breweries can manage and utilize their production volumes more effectively, ensuring that no batch goes to waste and optimizing their output.

**Techniques and Practices:**

- **Post-Fermentation Blending:** Most commonly, blending occurs after the fermentation process is complete. Brewers taste and evaluate different tanks of sake, then combine them in varying proportions to achieve the desired flavor profile.
- **Adjustment Blending:** Sometimes, blending is used to adjust specific characteristics of the sake, such as acidity or sweetness.
- **Cost-Efficiency Blending:** In some cases, blending involves combining higher-grade sake with lower-grade batches to create a more affordable product without sacrificing too much quality.

### Assemblage in HEAVENSAKE

**Definition and Concept:**
Assemblage, as introduced by HEAVENSAKE, particularly through the expertise of Régis Camus, refers to the **meticulous and artistic blending of different sake to create a unique, harmonious, and complex flavor profile.** This approach borrows from the traditions of Champagne making, where the cellar master blends various wines to produce a superior final product.

- **Artistic Craftsmanship:** Assemblage is seen as a form of artistry, focusing on enhancing and elevating the inherent qualities of each sake component rather than merely standardizing taste or reducing costs.
- **Complexity and Harmony:** The goal of assemblage is to create a sake that is more than the sum of its parts, offering a complex and balanced flavor profile that can't be achieved through simple blending techniques.
- **Quality Focus:** Unlike traditional blending, which might include cost-saving measures, assemblage prioritizes the highest quality ingredients and the unique characteristics they bring to the final product.

**Historical Inspiration:**

- **Champagne Tradition:** The concept of assemblage is deeply rooted in the traditions of Champagne making, where it is used to craft the most exquisite and balanced wines. Régis Camus brings this expertise to HEAVENSAKE, applying similar principles to sake production.
- **Innovation in Sake:** While traditional Japanese sake brewing has utilized blending for practical purposes, the introduction of assemblage represents a modern and innovative approach, aiming to redefine and elevate the sake drinking experience.

### Conclusion

Blending in traditional Japanese sake making has primarily been about ensuring consistency, managing costs, and optimizing production volumes.

**Assemblage, on the other hand, is an artistic and quality-driven approach** introduced by HEAVENSAKE, focusing on creating a unique and superior sake by harmoniously combining different batches.

By highlighting these distinctions, HEAVENSAKE can educate consumers on the sophistication and craftsmanship that goes into their products, setting them apart from traditional sake brands that use blending primarily for uniformity and cost efficiency.

## Prominent Brewery Acceptance

Prominent breweries like Dassai, Tatenokawa and Urakasumi have recognized the beauty of Assemblage which laid the ground for the Heavensake project. They all had one condition: **The Heavensake creations had to be clearly distinct from their existing products.**

Their approval underscores the power of blending. With Tatenokawa CEO, Junpei-san, even hailing Régis as one of the "Gods of Sake."

Régis needs on average **three days** to find the perfect result. He starts by categorising each sake by taste profile. The process begins by choosing a base to start from, followed by the addition of fruity and floral notes from other sake, and finally focusing on achieving the desired length.

The style, envisioned by Régis Camus, is characterised by its **fresh spring flower aromas and silky mouthfeel**, appealing especially to wine enthusiasts.
