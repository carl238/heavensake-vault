# Visual Identity & Symbols

The complete visual philosophy of HEAVENSAKE — the aesthetic north star, the key symbols, and the design rules that govern every image, bottle, and activation. All creative decisions must pass through "The Carl Filter."

---

## The Aesthetic North Star

**"Stanley Kubrick meets Daft Punk"**

This is the definitive four-word brief for HEAVENSAKE's visual identity. It means:

- **Kubrick:** High-contrast. Precise. Minimal. Psychologically charged. Imagery that holds tension. Composition over chaos.
- **Daft Punk:** Futuristic. Mysterious. Masked. Cultural currency. Not mainstream — culturally plugged-in. Timeless cool without nostalgia.

Combined: **High-contrast minimalism with cultural heat.** The aesthetic feels expensive without announcing it. It is art, not advertising.

---

## The Three Key Symbols

### 1. The Torii Gate

The Torii gate is the brand's primary sacred symbol. In Japanese culture, a Torii marks the threshold between the mundane and the sacred — the passage from ordinary to divine. For HEAVENSAKE, it represents:

- The threshold between the physical world and transcendence ("Pure Elevation")
- The Franco-Japanese crossing — two cultures meeting at a gate
- The invitation: crossing into a different world of taste and experience

**Used in:** Monaco F1 anniversary décor (LED + washi paper + plexiglass Torii gate centerpiece), 10-Year Anniversary T-shirts (Torii Gate graphic on chest), brand events as a visual anchor.

**IP note:** Carl flagged concern that the Torii gate design for Monaco must be legally protected. See [[Legal & Compliance]].

### 2. The Hexagon

A secondary symbol appearing in brand materials. The hexagon represents:

- Structure within nature (honeycomb — precision and harmony)
- The six sides mirroring the six (or seven) expressions in the product range
- Geometric order meeting organic beauty — the same tension as Franco-Japanese Assemblage

### 3. The Bottle as "A Brush Full of Ink"

The HEAVENSAKE bottle is described internally as "a brush full of ink" — an object that carries potential creative expression. It is not a container; it is an instrument. This framing applies to product photography and storytelling:

- The bottle should always appear as an active object, not a passive prop
- Pouring, rotating, liquid-filled — never static on a white background
- The Arita ceramic Prestige bottle extends this concept: mud-cast, iron-infused glaze — an art object as much as a vessel

## The "Papa Aesthetics" Ban

The following visual directions are **BANNED** at HEAVENSAKE. This is called "papa aesthetics" — the aesthetic of a previous generation that communicates tradition as nostalgia rather than living heritage.

| Banned Element | Why It's Banned |
|---------------|----------------|
| Dusty calligraphy | Evokes souvenir shop, not cultural authority |
| Wax seals | Historical, not contemporary |
| Mount Fuji as wallpaper | Japanese cliché without a French twist |
| Zen platitudes on packaging | Belongs on a tea bag |
| Cherry blossoms without context | Only acceptable in the [[Sakura Prestige Campaign]] with the "Pink Resonance" framing |
| Ochoko (cylindrical ceramic cup) | Functionally banned — removes 30–40% of aroma; use Wine Glass only |

**The test:** Does this image make HEAVENSAKE look like a 1990s sake brand or a 2026 global cultural luxury brand? If the former — delete it.

## High-Contrast Minimalism — The Visual Rules

1. **Light and purity are the foundation.** Bright, clean backgrounds. Shadows that create drama, not murkiness.
2. **One visual subject per frame.** The bottle, the glass, the pour, the person — not all together.
3. **Color palette:** Black, white, deep ink, pale gold, and the specific pink of the Sakura Prestige ("sanded pink" matte ceramic). No busy color fields.
4. **Typography:** Clean, modern. Sans-serif or razor-precision serif. No decorative script fonts.
5. **Video aesthetic:** Slow-motion liquid, macro detail (bubbles, vapor, condensation), atmospheric lighting. Inspired by high-end fragrance and fashion film.

## Cultural Reference Framework

Every visual must have a Franco-Japanese lens — Japanese references must carry a French twist, or they become cliché.

**Approved cultural juxtapositions:**
- Ink-brush tradition + minimalist European photography
- Ceramic artisanship + modernist architecture
- Japanese brewery ritual + Champagne cellar master formality
- Tokyo street culture + Monaco luxury setting
- Kinbaku (Shibari) art + French fashion film aesthetic (see [[Kinoko Hajime]] collaboration)

**Never:** Japanese visual culture in isolation (without the French gaze transforming it).

## Arita Porcelain Lab — The Material Language of the Prestige Line

[[Matsumoto-san]] at [[Arita Porcelain Lab]] produces the sculptural ceramic vessels for the Prestige range using:
- **Mud casting** — hand-formed, not machine-pressed
- **Iron-infused glazes** — creates organic surface variation; no two bottles are identical
- **Hand-drilling** — each bottle is a unique piece
- **"Sanded pink" matte finish** — for the Sakura Prestige/Hanami Prestige

The Prestige bottle is explicitly framed as an **art object**, not just packaging. It belongs in a gallery as much as on a table.
