# Carl's Leadership Philosophy

Carl's leadership style is direct, relational, and ruthlessly honest — with himself as much as with others. The key principles below are drawn from the moments where his management thinking is most explicit.

---

## "3 Stars to 5 Stars" — Growth Expectation

Carl currently rates team output at "3 stars" and aims for "5 stars" within 6 months. (Call 30)

This is the declared growth target for [[Claire Senda]] and the broader team. Performance is not a binary pass/fail — it is a progression from "good enough" to "remarkable." The gap between 3 and 5 stars is:
- Proactivity vs. responsiveness
- Closing items before opening new ones
- Delivering outputs that require no explanation or correction

---

## Daily 6pm WhatsApp Report

Claire sends a daily WhatsApp report to Carl at 6pm — digestible, prioritized by importance, with links to outputs. (Call 12)

This is not optional and not occasional. It is the operational heartbeat.

Carl's morning priority: "What works best for me is to wake up and have an understanding of what is happening out there with my team." (Call 30)

---

## Brand Manager vs. Assistant — The Core Distinction

> "Either you are a brand manager and you have that intelligence and that drive... Or you are an assistant." (Call 28)

A brand manager coordinates, solves problems, manages a network of creatives, and executes campaigns. A brand manager does NOT tape boxes or stand in post office lines. This distinction defines [[Claire Senda]]'s growth path and the standard for anyone in a brand role.

---

## Taste as a Core Competency

> "I don't think that Gloria has a great eye and great taste, so she cannot be a brand manager. I told her this." (Call 33)

For HEAVENSAKE, brand management requires aesthetic judgment — not just organizational skill. This is non-negotiable for anyone stewarding the brand. The Gloria example is the clearest statement of this principle:
- Good execution skills, lacks aesthetic sensitivity
- Can coordinate, cannot curate
- Cannot be a brand manager — Carl told her directly

---

## Close Items Before Opening New Ones

> "You love opening stuff and have these great ideas, but it's all about the actual closing. If you are just throwing a nice little things, you have all your things, then it becomes overwhelming." (Call 13)

This is an operational discipline Carl enforces rigorously. Always identify what is "must" vs. "nice to have" before committing resources. (Call 18)

The principle: **finishing is harder than starting, and finishing matters more.**

---

## The Relief Test for Team Decisions

Carl has a single, powerful heuristic for determining whether someone should stay on the team:

> "One interesting test is if somebody were to call you and say, 'I am quitting. I have to.' How sad would you truly be that person quit? How much would you fight to try to keep that person on board? Be like, 'Okay, no, no, no. I don't care. Double your salary, whatever.' If your answer is, 'I wouldn't really care. A part of me would actually be relieved' — you have a theoretically toxic negative person in your team because the actual cost of that, even if you don't realize it, the difference in your output in your speed in your focus is actually massive." (Call 28)

The insight: the hidden tax that underperformers impose on team momentum is always higher than it appears.

## Vendor Management Framework

> "You have to test people bit by bit, challenge by challenge. And yeah, if somebody can handle a hundred thousand dollar budget a year, then he should have it. But first he will have the way to prove you." (Call 28)

Framework for every vendor and contractor:

1. **Never scale a vendor before they've proven themselves on small tasks.** Test bit by bit. (Call 28)
2. **Vendors must justify pricing deviations from market rates.** (Call 19)
3. **Red flag:** Vendors who disappear when asked to justify quotes. (Call 19)
4. **Give honest feedback.** "Give the people honest feedback." (Call 11)
5. **Watch out for "pro hustlers"** — vendors who use AI to sound smarter in meetings. "Grill vendors with specific, prepared questions; use AI to generate trick questions." (Call 28)
6. **Watch for vendors inflating prices with hidden extras.** Fees should be all-inclusive. Administrative fees, communication fees, and small add-ons on top of an agreed creative fee are unacceptable. (Call 37)

## Budget Philosophy

- **Fair pricing explained, not demanded:** "If somebody quotes me 500, another one 2000, I need to understand the technical differences to make a decision." (Call 19)
- **Reward creative initiative.** If someone generates a cool idea that adds value: "you could charge $300 of creative and design fee or concept fee on top of what you're doing." (Call 11)
- **AI video production benchmark:** $2,000–$3,000 for cinematic quality. Anything more requires strong justification. (Call 37)
- **Professional photography minimum:** ~€160–200 per product shot; €480 for three shots is a good deal for an €800 product. (Call 13)

## Honesty as Culture

Carl explicitly invites the people he works with to challenge him:

> "I want to work with people that understand that and share that in a way that we can be like, okay, you know, let's be honest with each other. You're also allowed to confront me if you think, look, this is delusional, but I can show you the examples that I base myself on." (Call 33)

This is not just a preference — it is a deliberate hiring criterion. People who tell Carl what he wants to hear are not useful; people who push back with evidence are what he is building toward.

---

## Outcome-Based Compensation as a Principle

Carl's preference for outcome-based pay structures (% of revenue rather than hourly rates) reflects a management philosophy: align incentives with results.

The alternative to hourly billing — a percentage of ad revenue generated — is the model he wants to pilot. This extends across the team: variable compensation tied to performance, not seniority.

---

## The Junior + AI > Expensive Specialist Equation

Carl's most operationally disruptive management insight:

For community management, content production, data research, and outreach tasks: a 25-year-old who is AI-fluent, curious, and hungry will outperform a senior specialist who works manually. Cost: ~€2,000/month.

> "What I need is a 25 year old guy that is tech savvy but not a programmer. He also understands that it's probably not gonna be a very successful thing unless you already have the relationships… If you do not understand how to interact [with AI tools], it's the same as not knowing how to use email." (Call 33)

This principle is actively shaping HEAVENSAKE's 2026 team design: fewer expensive generalists, more junior AI-augmented operators with specific roles.

## Performance Culture

> "In a world where you see that somebody is so well organized, so precise, so proactive, so under control that they actually outperform and out-deliver their salary... that is amazing." (Call 33)

Salary and title are tied to demonstrated output quality, speed, and problem-solving — not tenure or hours worked.
