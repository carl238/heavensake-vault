# Carl's Leadership Philosophy

Carl's leadership style is direct, relational, and ruthlessly honest — with himself as much as with others. The key principles below are drawn from the moments where his management thinking is most explicit.

---

## The Relief Test for Team Decisions

Carl has a single, powerful heuristic for determining whether someone should stay on the team:

> "One interesting test is if somebody were to call you and say, 'I am quitting. I have to.' How sad would you truly be that person quit? How much would you fight to try to keep that person on board? Be like, 'Okay, no, no, no. I don't care. Double your salary, whatever.' If your answer is, 'I wouldn't really care. A part of me would actually be relieved' — you have a theoretically toxic negative person in your team because the actual cost of that, even if you don't realize it, the difference in your output in your speed in your focus is actually massive."
> — *2026-02-09 Marketing Alignment — Weekly Team Meeting*

The insight here is not just about negative people — it's about the hidden tax that underperformers impose on team momentum. Carl is explicit: the cost of keeping someone who would not be missed is always higher than it appears.

## Accountability Before Comfort

In the September 2025 operational crisis, Carl made the decision to fire Sara Vergani (operations coordinator) and restructure the operations team. The Italian-language summary from that meeting captures the core decision: systemic failures in shipping, damaged relationships with Japanese breweries (particularly Niizawa), and poor decision-making warranted not a performance plan but a clean break.

The new CEO at the time was to take over operations, with Carl refocusing on brand and marketing — a structural acknowledgment that he had been stretched too thin across both domains.

The June 2025 voice note on operations reveals a similar pattern: Michele was being considered to take over operations while Sara transitioned to a sales role or left entirely. The key lesson: when operational clarity breaks down, the solution is structural accountability, not more meetings.

## Who Gets to Be on the Team

Carl's profile for the people he wants around him is precise:

> "You want every single person on your team to be somebody that you enjoy working with, have a flow with. They're reliable, they understand, they make you understand things that you do not understand."
> — *2026-02-09 Marketing Alignment — Weekly Team Meeting*

The anti-profile — what he will not tolerate:

> "I was not on brand, he was not sharp, he was not fast. Total, total failure… He was the typical guy that's going to get crushed by the next generation of guys that are going to come in with the AI tools."
> — *2026-03-16 Prestige 3 Launch Strategy — Carl + Claire* (on Giancarlo)

Carl checked Giancarlo's emails and calendars before confirming the exit, and found he was working part-time. His reaction: relief, not anger.

## Honesty as Culture

Carl explicitly invites the people he works with to challenge him:

> "I want to work with people that understand that and share that in a way that we can be like, okay, you know, let's be honest with each other. You're also allowed to confront me if you think, look, this is delusional, but I can show you the examples that I base myself on."
> — *2026-03-18 AI Marketing Strategy — Carl + Jakub*

This is not just a preference — it is a deliberate hiring criterion. People who tell Carl what he wants to hear are not useful; people who push back with evidence are what he is building toward.

---

## Outcome-Based Compensation as a Principle

Carl's preference for outcome-based pay structures (% of revenue rather than hourly rates) reflects a management philosophy: align incentives with results.

In the Jakub engagement discussion, he was explicit that €100/hour (€16K/month) makes no sense for HEAVENSAKE. The alternative — a percentage of ad revenue generated — is the model he wants to pilot. This extends across the team: variable compensation tied to performance, not seniority.

---

## The Junior + AI > Expensive Specialist Equation

Carl's most operationally disruptive management insight:

For community management, content production, data research, and outreach tasks: a 25-year-old who is AI-fluent, curious, and hungry will outperform a senior specialist who works manually. Cost: ~€2,000/month. Output: equivalent to a €15,000/month agency.

> "What I need is a 25 year old guy that is tech savvy but not a programmer. He also understands that it's probably not gonna be a very successful thing unless you already have the relationships… If you do not understand how to interact [with AI tools], it's the same as not knowing how to use email."
> — *2026-03-18 AI Marketing Strategy — Carl + Jakub*

This principle is actively shaping HEAVENSAKE's 2026 team design: fewer expensive generalists, more junior AI-augmented operators with specific roles.

## The 6-Month Evaluation Framework

Carl evaluates people on explicit timeframes. Claire Senda's role as brand manager is a "6-month evaluation period." Zak Gross was given a 3-month performance window in December 2025. Giancarlo was given the opportunity to demonstrate value but ultimately failed the test.

The framework is fair (clear expectations, real time given) and decisive (when the verdict is reached, action follows).
